MLB Home Run Derby VR

I first started on the club/championship version of the game which was used by the MLB clubs to give stadium goers the experience of hitting dingers in their favourite stadium, then worked on the retail version for distribution on Steam, Meta and Playstation stores. For the club version, along with minor features, fixes and updates, I worked on an automated bat calibration flow that replaced the old trial-and-error manual system with a 2-button automatic system, increasing the speed and precision of the bat calibration.

For the retail version, I created the original prototypes and concepts for the updated main menu that better utilized VR interactivity, replacing conventional point-and-click UI menus with objects that could be grabbed, thrown, worn, etc.. I added room for social multiplayer aspects such as trophies, customization, and fidget toys such as food and drinks, including a custom soda tap to fill your glass with soda concoctions. Alongside the menu rework, I created the progression and equipment systems to allow players to show off their playtime and proficiency, as well as customize various aspects of their play to their liking with bat skins and stickers, emotes to communicate with other players, and different furniture to decorate their personal clubhouse with.

The Last Sky

My second major project, The Last Sky is a narrative driven action platformer with a unique movement system and shifting gameplay style. There was a ton of technical debt on the project from overuse of the Playmaker addon, which became my first task as I reworked most of the game's systems to be more usable and debug-able for both programmers writing C# and artists using Playmaker.

My biggest contribution to The Last Sky was a total rework and overhaul of the dialogue and story system, in which we moved from a simplistic and messy array of lines to a dialogue tree structure. The writers were using a third-party software called CeltX to write the dialogue, so I also created the import workflow to convert the CeltX output JSON into the in-engine dialogue trees, which saved not only dialogue but also any story flags or player choices and outcomes. All the lines in this dialogue system were also automatically stored in the project's I2 Localization sheet to allow for easy localizing to other languages.

Wings of Wanitu

Wings of Wanitu was my final major project at Little Guy Games. It is a novel VR flight combat game that combines realistic vehicle flight with rogue-lite elements to create an immersive VR flight experience with immense replayability and creativity. I was responsible for most of the game's design, including the rogue-lite systems and their in-game implementations, as well as player interaction and movement.

Wings of Wanitu had a very small team, so most of what I designed was also implemented by me. This includes flight physics, vehicle controls, weapon handling, balancing, the upgrade system, weapon selection, enemy and director AI, and player and stage progression. The upgrade system in particular took a lot of work, as it is based around triggered effects that were also able to chain into eachother. It was necessarily to prevent infinite trigger loops, as well as manually disable any synergies that may have been overpowered from the start.
